Sports

I figured out I was good at sports when I was about six years old.

I don't remember a single moment when it became clear that I was good at sports. No coach pulling me aside. No slow motion Disney movie event. Neither of my parents had really played sports. My dad's extracurriculars centered around music, and my mom had grown up a generation too early, before girls really had the chance to play. And yet, when I was in first grade, my dad, for reasons I still don't understand, signed me and a bunch of my friends up for the local recreational soccer league.

We were called the Argonauts, after Jason and the Greek myth (the Greek Myths book was a staple of bedtime reading during my childhood). My mom made a "golden fleece" that was awarded by Coach aka Dad to to a standout player from that game. The golden fleece looked awesome but was scratchy and uncomfortable. We were playing in a first- and second-grade league, and we were all first graders. So our team of 6 year olds about 17 percent less physically developed than our 7 year old competition. We were terrible. Not charmingly bad. Just bad. We lost every game. We scored one goal all year, in a six-to-one loss that felt like a moral victory.

In between seasons, we played soccer at recess on the asphalt, with two sets of slightly askew monkey bars as the goals. And lo and behold, by second grade we were better. By third grade, now called the Titans, we were good. And for me personally, something had clicked. I was fast. I was strong. I could control the ball. And I scored a lot of goals.

Being an athlete quickly became the most important part of my identity. Within the world of a sports, the feedback loop is so clean. Clear rules. Clear definitions of success. Instant respect from peers and adults if you are good. You ran harder, you practiced more, you got better. I had found a place where effort reliably turned into result. So much of life childhood, and later adulthood, I would struggle with uncertainty, ambling and searching for purpose. Just trying to understand what game I was actually playing. But inside the contained realm of a specific sports, the rules were clear.

I spent much of elementary school outside, playing with my next-door neighbor Jodie who was born about 9 weeks before me. We played variations of games in our front and backyards and on the cul-de-sac around the corner or the playground down the hill. Part of the fun was trying to adapt the game to fit into the confines of our space. The short hedge was the dodge ball net. The fence the soccer goal. We got better by playing, in the truest sense of the word. Jodie was part of the first cohort of girls that grew up playing soccer. She played on a soccer once shared a field with someone who would later be on the 1999 U.S. women's national team. At the time, none of that mattered.

I started playing baseball when I was 8. I was good at it quickly: pitching, playing shortstop, hitting home runs. When I was nine, by my memory—which I don't fully trust—I reached base thirty-six times in forty at-bats and struck out once. That sounds impossible, and it probably is. Detailed recaps of my weekend baseball game was my go-to journal subject matter in third grade. What's true is that I was a huge Oakland A's fan, back when Oakland still had teams to root for. My bedroom walls were covered with posters of Mark McGwire and Jose Canseco, before we knew what we would later learn about steroid use. I was devastated when Kirk Gibson hit that home run, with a corked bat, obviously, off Dennis Eckersley. I tried to time my swing to televised pitches in the living room, despite my mom yelling, "Don't swing a bat in the house!"

At my grandmother's house in rural Maine, my brother, cousins, and I built our own baseball field. We cleared it with a rusty scythe and a gas mower—I'm amazed nobody got hurt. We made fences out of chicken wire and old boards. We invented variations that let us play two-on-two, including a fake first baseman made of fence that you could throw the ball to. I am the oldest, which means I hit the most home runs. I watch one clear the fence we built ourselves and trot the bases, alone with the certainty of it. No one needs to explain what just happened. There is no ambiguity, no interpretation. The ball is gone. The bases are fixed. The meaning is self-evident.

When I was nine, I moved up into the under‑twelve baseball league and was the first pick in the little league draft. Coach Johnson knew me and really wanted me. At this point, I used baseball on Sundays as my reason to stop going to church. We had been attending a hippie Berkeley Congregationalist church. Baseball became my religion. I knew what was expected of me. I knew how to measure myself. I started collecting baseball cards and would religiously scour the sports pages every morning. I think that a reason I was so successful at math was because I spent cycles thinking about how to calculate the conditional probability of a run with two outs and a runner on second base.

I made all three varsity teams (soccer, basketball, baseball) as a freshman. I was already good at soccer and baseball, but more of a "project" with basketball. I hadn't played in an organized basketball game until 8th grade, and I was awful when I started. I was tall, but in the middle of a 12 inches in one year growth spurt so not very coordinated. I remember staying late at school because my parents had to work and having the gym to myself and running back and forth playing one on zero and shooting hundreds of shots. I made the varsity team in as a freshman in high school because of potential, even though I was still bad. I dunked for the first time the summer after ninth grade. I played summer leagues. We got a hoop at home and I would shoot while procrastinating homework or for a few minutes in the morning while waiting for everyone else to leave the house to get in the car to drive to school.

In the first soccer game of my soccer fall season, I scored a goal in the first half. But then in the second half, I broke my ankle. Or, more accurately, my ankle was broken by a goalie diving recklessly onto it during a loose ball scramble in front of the goal. I spent six miserable weeks in a cast that smelled terrible, hopping around on my left leg, which happened to be my power leg for basketball. I  spent afternoons just shooting free throws in a walking cast because I couldn't do anything else, and was hitting dozens in a row by the time By the time the cast came off, I could hop and dunk a tennis ball. In my first basketball game back from injury, midway through basketball season, I scored seventeen points in less than half the game. Injury, it turned out, could be folded into the logic of improvement. Even pain had a place in the story, as long as it eventually resolved into progress.

By junior year, I was scoring sixteen or seventeen points a game and was co-MVP with Marlon on a team that was 20-someting and 5. By senior year we were one of the best (small school) teams in the state.

Senior year, it stops working. State playoffs. I grab a rebound, put it back in, come down on someone's foot. I roll my ankle—Loss bad. I try to stay in, but I can't put weight on it. I watch from the sideline as we lose by one, fifty to forty-nine. Our dream of a state championship—very achievable, I think, if I hadn't gotten hurt—ends there. I'm in a cast again. My career is over.

I came back for the last half of the baseball season and hit a grand slam in my first game, but I had lost my love for the sport. Baseball, I realized, is a hard game for a certain temperament. You act for a few minutes and then sit with your failures for hours. I had once struck out in a pivotal at-bat and thrown my helmet in anger, hitting another kid in the head. I'm not proud of that. Basketball was different. Even when your shot is off, you can play defense, rebound, make a pass. The game keeps moving.

I was recruited in the old way: handwritten letters, envelopes, meat-market camps in towns I never returned to. I went to Stanford thinking I might walk on. I have no idea where this confidence came from. I was a six-foot-two guard from a small private school in California. This was delusional, and I figured that out within a year.

I transferred to Williams and played on a Division III team that went to the Final Four twice. I played behind the best player in the country, a two-time national player of the year. I rarely cracked the rotation. I scored double digits once, in a game we won by thirty-five. I had a lot of DNPs. But I wore the uniform. Played hard. Made friends. And definitely got much better at basketball. And I can look back now without "what if" regrets.

My greatest contribution to the program, according to an assistant coach, was the best dunking performance in its history. One February afternoon after practice, well-conditioned but well-rested, I started throwing down dunk after dunk—a reverse, a tomahawk, a left-handed leaner. The assistants ran upstairs to get the head coach. The whole team cheered. Instagram didn't exist. There is no evidence this happened. You'll have to take my word for it. Mostly, I still didn't play.

I stopped my senior year to focus on academics and wrote a thesis. I gained weight. I got out of shape. A year later, I go for a jog with a woman I've just started dating. She runs behind me, gently pushing me up a hill while I struggle to make it two miles. She's now my wife.

What surprised me was how much joy sports brought me after ambitions had faded. In my twenties and thirties, sports gave me more than they ever had before. Pickup basketball with a regular crew of neighborhood dads and a few non-dad interlopers. Wednesday night tennis and beers after. Soccer games at ten at night under bad lights with people I barely knew but high-fived enthusiastically anyway. No crowds. No stats. No newspaper clippings. None of the glory that I now suspect fueled a lot of my earlier motivation.

What was left was repetition and sweat and the quiet satisfaction of belonging. For an introvert, it was the perfect social life. You don't have to talk much. You just play. Try to win. But not so seriously that you don't laugh when things go wrong. It's an hour or two where the expectations are obvious and the feedback immediate. You don't have to wonder if you're doing the right thing or becoming the right version of yourself. You're either open or you're not. The shot goes in or it doesn't.

In Boston, I spent a decade playing basketball at Curtis Hall, an old gym with a track that wrapped around the court and blocked the corner threes. This was probably the height of my game—my understanding had matured, and my body still cooperated. I must have hit thousands of three-pointers there, including a fair number of game winners.

Once, a new guy sells out trying to shut me down. I come off a pin-down, hit a three to win it, and he runs straight at me. For a split second I think he's going to hit me. Instead he yells, "That's a great shot, man. Great shot" and gave me one of those shove in the chest that is really a sign of respect.

I started playing a lot of tennis. Old man soccer leagues. Older man (over 48!) soccer leagues 

As I aged, I adapted. I embraced what I thought of as the Andre Iguodala phase of my game—Warriors era, for those keeping track. I stopped being the alpha taking big shots and started caring more about whether the right person took them. I found satisfaction in setting a screen that freed a shooter, or driving baseline just to drop a bounce pass to a cutter who hadn't even realized he was open yet. It wasn't dominance, but it was fluency. I still knew what I was doing out there. There is a particular comfort in competence without ambition.

Then there was pain. Not the familiar soreness you expect after a hard game, but something heavier and more persistent. I tore my meniscus. MRIs showed advanced arthritis. One doctor told me I'd almost certainly need a knee replacement—just not yet. Too young, he said, because the replacements only last so long and we don't want to have to do another one. Come back in a decade. I told him I'd pencil it in.

Covid shut things down when I was 43. And then I got cancer when I was 45. Two years of treatment, two years away from the court. When I come back at 46, I'm bald from chemo. I can't get by people. I can't create space. My body hurts in unfamiliar ways. The game hasn't changed. I have. The joy thins out. Some days it comes back, just long enough to remind me what it used to feel like. Most days it doesn't. The hardest part isn't the pain itself—it's the way it interrupts the old bargain, the one where you keep showing up and the game keeps meeting you halfway.

I wouldn't say it's over. It's just profoundly different. This is a cliché. We all go through this. I'm not special—just the latest in a long line of middle-aged men who thought they'd be different. I mostly play doubles tennis now. Ten years ago I thought doubles was boring because you didn't even break a sweat. Now that's pretty much all I play. I still turn my nose up at pickleball. But I've started noticing the courts. I tell myself I'm just curious.

I introduced my daughters to sports when they were young and have loved playing with them in the yard, coaching them when they were little. When my daughter Nina was in eighth grade, we went for a jog and I just couldn't keep pace. It was humbling, but I wasn't annoyed to be losing. I was proud of her. But maybe a little sad for me.

A year later, she's a ninth grader in a big game. Last minute. She cradles the ball with her chest and heads it over the goalie. I feel the same way watching her: pride first, then something quieter.

I sometimes wonder what my life would look like without sports. They gave me confidence long before I knew how to manufacture it on my own. They gave me a kind of legitimacy—a résumé that helped open doors, credibility that made certain paths feel available. In ways that make me slightly uncomfortable to admit, I benefited from being good at games.

And in the end, a lot of that came down to luck. I was born into a body that happened to cooperate with the games I loved. I didn't earn that body. I didn't choose it. I lived in it for a while. And then, like everyone else, I'm learning what it means to let it go.
